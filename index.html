Understanding the Condition

What is Tension Myositis Syndrome?

Tension Myositis Syndrome — TMS — is a term coined by Dr. John E. Sarno, a physician and professor at NYU Langone Medical Center, to describe a condition where the brain produces real, physical pain as a protective response to overwhelming emotional stress. The pain is not imagined. It is physiologically real. But its origin is psychological.

Sarno's central insight was that the brain reduces blood flow — and therefore oxygen — to muscles, nerves, and tendons in order to create a distraction. When the psyche is overwhelmed with emotions it cannot safely process — rage, grief, guilt, shame, deep anxiety — the unconscious mind generates physical pain to keep attention away from those feelings.

What makes TMS remarkable is how it resolves. Unlike most medical conditions, TMS responds profoundly to knowledge. When a patient genuinely accepts that their pain is psychologically generated — through true understanding — the brain loses its reason to maintain the symptom. Sarno called this the "knowledge cure."

The purpose of the pain is to divert attention from what's going on emotionally. Once you understand that, recovery can begin.
— Dr. John E. Sarno
Core Concepts

Three Key Principles

Sarno's work rests on three interconnected ideas. Understanding all three is the foundation of recovery.

01

The Emotional Origin

Pain originates from repressed emotions — especially unconscious rage — not from tissue damage or structural abnormalities. The body is the messenger of what the mind cannot speak.

02

Knowledge is the Cure

Understanding the psychological basis of TMS removes the brain's power to maintain the symptom. Insight, not surgery, is the pathway to recovery.

03

Resume Living Fully

Return to all normal physical activity without fear. Avoidance reinforces the pain cycle and signals to the brain that its strategy is working.

Sarno's Daily Practice

The Daily Reminders

Sarno asked his patients to read these reminders every day — a practice of keeping the mind anchored to the psychological reality of TMS rather than slipping back into fear.

  1. 1The pain is caused by TMS, not a structural abnormality.
  2. 2The direct reason for the pain is mild oxygen deprivation.
  3. 3TMS is a harmless condition, caused by repressed emotions.
  4. 4The principal emotion is unconscious rage.
  5. 5TMS exists only to distract my attention from the emotions.
  6. 6Since my back is basically normal, there is nothing to fear.
  7. 7Therefore, physical activity is not dangerous.
  8. 8I must resume all normal physical activity.
  9. 9I will not be intimidated by the pain.
  10. 10I will shift my attention from the pain to emotional issues.
  11. 11I intend to be in control — not my unconscious mind.
  12. 12I must think psychological at all times, not physical.

Clearing the Doubts

Common Questions & Doubts

Sarno pointed out that imaging abnormalities are extremely common in people with zero pain. Studies consistently show that large proportions of asymptomatic adults have disc herniations, bulging discs, and other MRI findings. Meanwhile, many people in severe chronic pain have completely normal scans. Structural findings are often incidental — present in the image, but not the cause of the suffering.

Sarno never advised ignoring serious medical conditions. He strongly recommended ruling out dangerous pathology — fractures, tumors, infections, cauda equina syndrome — with a qualified physician. TMS is a diagnosis arrived at after serious pathology has been excluded. If your doctor has identified a genuinely dangerous structural condition, that deserves appropriate medical attention.
Yes — in fact, chronicity is one of the hallmarks of TMS. True structural injuries heal within weeks or a few months. Pain that persists for years, migrates between locations, and waxes and wanes with stress is far more consistent with a mind-body process than with ongoing structural damage.
Some people recover simply by reading Sarno's books. Others benefit from working with a TMS-informed therapist, particularly if they carry significant trauma or deeply ingrained fear-avoidance patterns. There is no single right path. The common thread is moving attention from the physical to the psychological.
Sarno expanded his theory to include conditions he called "TMS equivalents": tension headaches, migraines, irritable bowel syndrome, fibromyalgia, skin conditions, repetitive stress injuries, anxiety, and others. As awareness of one symptom grows and it loses its distracting power, the unconscious may shift to another — a pattern Sarno called "the symptom imperative."
